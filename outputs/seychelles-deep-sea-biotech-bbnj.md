# Deep-Sea Zones for Blue Biotechnology in the Seychelles EEZ and BBNJ Benefit-Sharing Governance

**Research Brief | July 2026**

---

## Research Question

*What does existing geomorphological and bathymetric data reveal about priority deep-sea zones for blue biotechnology potential within the Seychelles EEZ, and what governance capacities are required to translate that spatial prioritisation into enforceable sovereign benefit-sharing claims under the BBNJ Agreement?*

---

## Executive Summary

The Seychelles EEZ (1.37 million km²) contains a geomorphologically diverse deep-sea landscape—centred on the Amirante Trench, the Aldabra seamount bases, the Coco de Mer Seamount Ridge, and the abyssal plains flanking the Mascarene Plateau—that constitutes an underexplored frontier for blue biotechnology. Fewer than 25% of these waters have been mapped at high resolution, yet existing multibeam surveys and ecological surveys reveal benthic communities of likely exceptional endemism in the bathyal and abyssal zones. Commercially relevant deep-sea organisms—thermostable enzyme producers, antimicrobial peptide synthesisers, exopolysaccharide-producing microbes—remain largely uncharacterised in the Western Indian Ocean. Translating this spatial priority into enforceable benefit-sharing claims requires Seychelles to close four parallel governance gaps: (1) domestic ABS legislation covering EEZ bioprospecting; (2) scientific capacity for in-situ sampling and genomic characterisation; (3) institutional linkage between the Seychelles Marine Spatial Plan (SMSP), the Seychelles Fisheries Authority (SFA), and BBNJ implementation bodies; and (4) active engagement with the BBNJ Clearing-House Mechanism and patent surveillance. Seychelles' position as the first African state to ratify the BBNJ Agreement and co-chair of the High Ambition Coalition gives it unusual diplomatic leverage, but that leverage cannot be converted into material benefit-sharing flows without corresponding domestic institutional investment.

## 1. Geomorphological and Bathymetric Context

### 1.1 The Seychelles EEZ as a Deep-Sea Province

The Seychelles EEZ spans approximately 1.37 million km² of the western Indian Ocean and encompasses a geological heritage unique in the global ocean: the ancient Precambrian granitic Seychelles Bank, overlain by and flanked by younger volcanic and carbonate structures. This creates a bathymetric mosaic unusual for an island-state EEZ, ranging from shallow banks (<50 m) to abyssal plains (>4,300 m), with structurally complex intermediate zones of high biological interest.

The Seychelles Marine Spatial Plan (SMSP) Initiative, launched in 2014, produced the most systematic spatial analysis of the EEZ to date. Its Marxan with Zones analysis incorporated 87 biodiversity and ecological features alongside deep seafloor geomorphology layers, enabling zone delimitation into three categories: Zone 1 (High Biodiversity Protection), Zone 2 (Medium Biodiversity Protection and Sustainable Use), and Zone 3 (Multiple Use). Notably, the initial planning milestones prioritised deep-water zoning because most existing marine activities were concentrated in waters shallower than 200 m—leaving the deeper zones as the primary planning challenge.

### 1.2 Key Geomorphological Features and Their Biotechnology Significance

**Amirante Trench and Passage (~4,000–5,200 m depth)**

The Amirante Trench is the deepest geomorphological feature within or immediately adjacent to the Seychelles EEZ, a northward-trending elongated trough whose free-air gravity signature closely resembles subduction-zone trenches of the western Pacific. Its tectonic origin—related to the collision of the Seychelles continental block with oceanic crust of the Mascarene Basin—makes it a candidate site for elevated fluid flux, chemosynthetic communities, and the cold-seep/hydrothermal conditions that consistently produce novel bioactive metabolites. Cold-seep microbiomes globally have yielded antimicrobial oligoindoles, polyene macrolides, and unique biosynthetic gene clusters absent from shallow-water equivalents. The Amirante Trench has not been systematically sampled for microbial or macrofaunal biodiversity, making it the highest-priority target for directed bioprospecting within the EEZ.

**Aldabra Group Seamount Bases (~3,800–4,300 m depth)**

The raised coral reefs of Aldabra, Assumption, Cosmoledo, and Astove sit atop seamounts approximately 4,000 m high, rising from a relatively flat seafloor at 4,000–4,300 m depth. The flanks of these seamounts—particularly in the bathyal zone (300–3,000 m)—exhibit the structural complexity and hard substrate necessary to support sponge gardens, cold-water coral communities, crinoid fields, and associated microbiomes that are a primary source of bioactive secondary metabolites. The recently completed study on geomorphological drivers of deeper reef habitats confirmed that sites with steep slopes and high terrain complexity host higher fish abundance and biomass, and assemblages at 120 m depth correlate positively with relief and structural complexity. This relationship is likely to extend into deeper bathyal zones, concentrating biodiverse communities on the steeper slope segments.

The SeyCCAT-funded investigation into marine predator connectivity between shallow and deep-sea ecosystems around Aldabra provides a foundation for understanding trophic linkages that can guide sampling strategy for deep biological communities.

**Coco de Mer Seamount Ridge**

Identified in the SMSP climate risk analysis as a distinct geomorphological feature in the north-western sector of the EEZ, the Coco de Mer Seamount Ridge represents an additional seamount chain with unexplored deep flanks and summit ecosystems. Seamounts globally are known for high endemism rates—each seamount functions as an isolated biological island, generating evolutionary divergence. The Coco de Mer Seamount Ridge's position in the north-western EEZ, exposed to different oceanographic conditions than the southern island groups, suggests a distinct biological community potentially harbouring compounds not found elsewhere.

**Fortune Bank and the Amirantes to Fortune Bank Zone**

The SMSP designated the Amirantes to Fortune Bank corridor as Zone 2 (Medium Biodiversity Protection and Sustainable Use). Fortune Bank marks the eastern boundary of the EEZ's highest sea surface temperature anomaly zone. The bathyal margins of the bank system, where the shallow carbonate platform gives way to deep slopes, constitute transition habitats of documented ecological richness and likely high undiscovered species richness.

**Abyssal Plains (~4,000–4,500 m)**

Over 77% of the Indian Ocean lies at abyssal depths. The abyssal plains within and adjacent to the Seychelles EEZ—largely flat but interrupted by the above-described structural features—host abyssal sediment communities including foraminifera, polychaetes, and microbial mats whose biotechnology relevance is increasingly recognised, particularly for extremozymes adapted to high pressure and low temperature. These communities remain entirely uncharacterised within the Seychelles EEZ.

### 1.3 The Mapping Gap

A 2024 OceanX expedition—conducted in partnership with the Republic of Seychelles, Seychelles Islands Foundation, Island Conservation Society, and SeyCCAT—mapped over 22,600 km² of previously unmapped seafloor using multibeam echo sounder technology and deployed Baited Remote Underwater Videos (BRUVs) at 300, 600, and 900 m depths. This represents a significant advance but covers a small fraction of the total EEZ deep-sea area. It is estimated that globally over 75% of the seafloor remains unmapped at high resolution; the Seychelles EEZ deep-sea is in that majority. Credible spatial prioritisation for bioprospecting—and subsequent benefit-sharing claims—requires this data gap to be systematically closed, making continued investment in multibeam mapping a foundational prerequisite.

## 2. Blue Biotechnology Potential

### 2.1 The Deep-Sea Genetic Resource Landscape

Deep-sea environments are now recognised as disproportionately productive sources of commercially valuable marine genetic resources. Analysis of global marine gene patent databases shows a significant concentration of patents associated with microbial species from extreme deep-sea environments, particularly hydrothermal vents and cold seeps. Relevant product categories include:

- **Thermostable and pressure-stable enzymes** (DNA polymerases, proteases, lipases) for industrial and diagnostic biotechnology
- **Antimicrobial peptides** from deep cold-seep-derived bacteria and fungi, including recent discoveries of antimicrobial oligoindoles from *Halomonas* strains
- **Exopolysaccharides (EPS)** from hydrothermal vent communities, with pharmaceutical and cosmetics applications
- **Biosynthetic gene clusters** encoding novel polyketides, non-ribosomal peptides, and terpenes
- **Photoprotective compounds** from mesophotic zone invertebrates adapted to dim light

Between 70–90% of marine species globally remain undescribed. The Indian Ocean has been less intensively sampled than the Atlantic or Pacific in deep bioprospecting campaigns, meaning the Western Indian Ocean—including the Seychelles EEZ—likely harbours a disproportionate share of undescribed biodiversity and associated novel chemistry.

### 2.2 Priority Depth Zones by Biotechnology Application

| Zone | Depth Range | Priority Feature | Biotechnology Target |
|------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Rariphotic-Upper Bathyal | 150–600 m | Aldabra seamount upper flanks, Coco de Mer Ridge | Sponge-associated microbes; cold-water coral compounds; antimicrobials |
| Mid-Bathyal | 600–2,000 m | Amirante Trench walls, seamount mid-flanks | Pressure-stable enzymes; deep coral secondary metabolites |
| Lower Bathyal–Abyssal Transition | 2,000–4,000 m | Amirante Trench lower flanks, seamount bases | Cold-seep microbiomes; biosynthetic gene clusters; novel polyketides |
| Hadal-adjacent abyssal | 4,000–5,200 m | Amirante Trench floor, abyssal plains | Extremozymes; pressure-adapted microbes; undescribed Archaea |

### 2.3 Five Priority Research Hypotheses

**H1 — Amirante Trench as primary bioprospecting target:** The Amirante Trench, as the deepest and most tectonically distinct feature within the Seychelles EEZ, has the highest prior probability of harbouring chemosynthetic communities (cold seeps, sulfidic sediments) that are globally associated with the greatest density of novel bioactive metabolites per unit area sampled.

**H2 — Mesophotic-bathyal transition gap as near-term priority:** The mesophotic-to-bathyal transition zone (150–600 m) around Aldabra Group seamounts represents the highest near-term discovery probability, given documented high terrain complexity, confirmed undescribed benthic invertebrate fauna from limited submersible surveys, and the established pattern of sponge and coral diversity at these depths in comparable seamount systems.

**H3 — Coco de Mer Seamount Ridge harbours genetically distinct communities:** Given its geomorphological isolation and distinct oceanographic position in the north-western EEZ, the Coco de Mer Seamount Ridge likely hosts microbial and invertebrate communities with greater genetic distance from shallow-water Indian Ocean assemblages than comparable features in other zones, increasing the novelty of biosynthetic gene clusters present.

**H4 — Digital Sequence Information (DSI) extraction will precede in-situ sampling for commercial development:** Given the rapid advance of ocean environmental DNA (eDNA) sequencing from water column samples, commercial bioprospectors are likely to derive DSI from the Seychelles deep-sea before Seychelles itself can conduct systematic collection missions. This makes DSI governance under BBNJ an urgent priority even before physical sampling capacity is established.

**H5 — Bathymetric mapping investment unlocks a multiplier effect on benefit-sharing claims:** Each additional km² of high-resolution multibeam mapping converts poorly-defined "open ocean" into documented geomorphological habitat, enabling Seychelles to make spatially precise notifications to the BBNJ Clearing-House Mechanism and to challenge misattributed research permissions.

## 3. Governance Capacities for Enforceable Benefit-Sharing Claims

### 3.1 The BBNJ Agreement Framework

The Agreement under UNCLOS on the Conservation and Sustainable Use of Marine Biological Diversity of Areas Beyond National Jurisdiction (BBNJ Agreement) was adopted on 19 June 2023 and entered into force on 17 January 2026, with Seychelles as one of the first ratifiers (fourth country globally, first in Africa). Its Part II establishes the regime for marine genetic resources (MGRs) and the fair and equitable sharing of benefits.

**Key provisions:**

- **Pre-collection notification:** Researchers collecting MGRs in areas beyond national jurisdiction (ABNJ) must upload pre-collection information to the Clearing-House Mechanism, generating a standardised BBNJ batch identifier that links the sample to subsequent utilisation records.
- **Non-monetary benefits:** Mandatory sharing of samples, research results, data, capacity building, and technology transfer.
- **Monetary benefits:** COP-reviewed modalities including milestone payments, revenue percentages, or tiered fees paid into a special fund. Developed country parties pay an additional 50% of assessed contributions into the fund (Article 14(6)).
- **SIDS provisions:** Special requirements of SIDS are to be given full recognition in Clearing-House Mechanism access, with dedicated programmes to reduce participation barriers.
- **Capacity-Building and Technology Transfer (CBTMT) Committee:** Institutionalised mandate to address structural inequalities.

**Critical Limitation — EEZ vs. ABNJ:**

A fundamental jurisdictional distinction shapes the entire benefit-sharing calculus. The BBNJ Agreement applies *only* to areas beyond national jurisdiction—the high seas and the international seabed area (the "Area"). The Seychelles EEZ, over which Seychelles holds sovereign rights under UNCLOS Articles 55–75, is *not* covered by BBNJ. MGRs collected within the Seychelles EEZ are governed exclusively by Seychelles domestic law and, where applicable, the CBD/Nagoya Protocol framework. This means:

- Resources from the Amirante Trench, Aldabra seamount flanks, and Coco de Mer Ridge within the EEZ are subject to Seychelles' own ABS regime, not BBNJ.
- Only resources collected in waters or on the seabed beyond the EEZ boundary trigger BBNJ obligations.
- The absence of robust domestic ABS legislation is therefore the more immediate enforcement gap than BBNJ implementation.

### 3.2 The Disclosure of Origin Gap

The most consequential structural deficiency in the BBNJ benefit-sharing architecture is the absence of any mandatory disclosure of origin requirement. Disclosure of origin—which would require patent applicants to identify the geographic source of any MGR used in an invention—was included in early draft treaty texts but deleted from the final instrument. Without it:

- The Clearing-House Mechanism's batch identifier system has no enforcement hook in intellectual property proceedings.
- A researcher can collect from ABNJ adjacent to Seychelles waters, publish genomic data, file patents based on that data, and Seychelles has no traceable link to assert.
- The benefit-sharing regime becomes aspirational rather than legally binding in commercial contexts.

This gap requires Seychelles to pursue parallel strategies: supporting disclosure of origin in WIPO Genetic Resources Treaty deliberations, advocating through the BBNJ COP for DSI traceability provisions, and investing in independent patent surveillance capacity.

### 3.3 Required Governance Capacities — A Prioritised Framework

#### Tier 1: Foundational (required before benefit-sharing claims become credible)

**1. Domestic ABS Legislation**
Seychelles lacks a standalone national Access and Benefit-Sharing law for MGRs within the EEZ. The Nagoya Protocol, to which Seychelles is a party via the CBD, requires a functional domestic ABS regime (prior informed consent, mutually agreed terms, benefit-sharing provisions) for genetic resources within national jurisdiction. Without this, foreign researchers can lawfully collect from EEZ waters without sharing benefits, regardless of BBNJ. Drafting and enacting this legislation—with a marine-specific bioprospecting permit regime administered by SFA or a dedicated office—is the single highest-priority governance action.

**2. Marine Scientific Research Permit System**
A functional, transparent, and enforceable permitting system for marine scientific research within the EEZ—distinguishing commercial bioprospecting from basic research and mandating benefit-sharing agreements as permit conditions—is necessary to intercept commercial MGR collection before it occurs without obligations.

#### Tier 2: Operational (required to act on rights)

**3. Deep-Sea Survey and Sampling Capacity**
Seychelles cannot make defensible discovery claims—or challenge misattributed samples—without demonstrated domestic scientific capacity to document what exists in its deep-sea. The 2024 OceanX partnership demonstrates a viable model: leveraging international research vessel access while embedding Seychelles scientists to develop in-country expertise and generate nationally attributed data. The SFA's Fisheries Policy 2024–2029 should be read alongside the BBNJ implementation agenda to ensure deep-sea research is explicitly mandated.

**4. Genetic Sequencing and Bioinformatics Capacity**
Commercial value in blue biotechnology increasingly flows through DSI—genomic sequences deposited in databases and used to develop synthetic biology products without any physical sample transfer. Seychelles needs at minimum a national reference collection, sequencing partnerships with established laboratories, and a data management system that links sequences to geographic collection points within the EEZ. This provides the evidentiary base for future benefit-sharing demands and DSI governance advocacy.

**5. Clearing-House Mechanism Participation**
Seychelles must establish a designated national focal point with technical capacity to monitor the BBNJ Clearing-House Mechanism: reviewing batch identifiers uploaded by researchers active in adjacent ABNJ, cross-referencing with its own survey data, and flagging collections that may include material from EEZ-adjacent zones where attribution is ambiguous.

#### Tier 3: Systemic (required to convert rights into sustained flows)

**6. Institutional Integration**
The SMSP Unit (in the Ministry of Environment, Climate, Energy and Natural Resources), the Seychelles Fisheries Authority, the Ministry responsible for BBNJ negotiations, the Attorney General's Office, and the Seychelles Conservation and Climate Adaptation Trust (SeyCCAT) currently operate without a formal coordination mechanism for MGR governance. A national MGR coordination committee—modelled on the National Biodiversity Authority models of India and South Africa—is needed to prevent regulatory fragmentation.

**7. Patent Surveillance and DSI Monitoring**
Seychelles needs ongoing monitoring of patent databases (particularly EPO, USPTO, WIPO PATENTSCOPE) for applications disclosing marine organism origins in or adjacent to the Seychelles EEZ or the Western Indian Ocean. This is technically feasible through keyword and geographic tag searches, increasingly AI-assisted, but requires institutional assignment of responsibility and adequate legal advisory capacity to pursue claims.

**8. Diplomatic and Coalition Leverage**
Seychelles' diplomatic position is its most immediate asset. As co-chair of the BBNJ High Ambition Coalition (with Palau and the EU) and first African BBNJ ratifier, Seychelles has convening power in the BBNJ COP. This should be used to: (a) push for mandatory disclosure of origin in the WIPO-BBNJ interface; (b) advocate for higher monetary benefit percentages in COP modality reviews; (c) lead the SIDS coordination bloc on Clearing-House Mechanism technical design; and (d) negotiate bilateral capacity-building agreements with states whose research vessels are active in the Western Indian Ocean.

## 5. Conclusions and Research Priorities

The available geomorphological and bathymetric record, despite its significant incompleteness, clearly identifies the **Amirante Trench** and the **bathyal flanks of the Aldabra and Coco de Mer seamount systems** as the priority zones for deep-sea blue biotechnology potential within the Seychelles EEZ. The combination of structural complexity, abyssal and bathyal depths, tectonic distinctiveness, and documented (if sparse) mesophotic biodiversity creates conditions analogous to the world's most productive deep-sea bioprospecting sites. However, over 75% of the deep-sea EEZ remains unmapped at actionable resolution, meaning the current spatial prioritisation is necessarily provisional.

The translation of this potential into enforceable sovereign benefit-sharing claims faces a dual jurisdictional structure: BBNJ governs the high seas beyond the EEZ, where the Clearing-House Mechanism and monetary benefit fund provide the operative mechanisms; but the EEZ itself—where most of the biotechnology-relevant features lie—requires domestic ABS legislation that does not yet exist in Seychelles. Closing this domestic legislative gap is the most urgent governance action. Seychelles' early BBNJ ratification and co-chair status provide exceptional diplomatic leverage in shaping the evolving BBNJ benefit-sharing modalities, particularly on DSI traceability and the disclosure of origin question, but this leverage will be wasted without parallel domestic institutional investment.
